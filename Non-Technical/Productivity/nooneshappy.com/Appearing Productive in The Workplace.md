# [Appearing Productive in The Workplace](https://nooneshappy.com/article/appearing-productive-in-the-workplace/)
* There is output-competence decoupling
  * In a previous era, the quality of work product was a reliable signal of the competence of the person who produced it
  * Now, work product reflects the competence of the underlying system
  * Users are now a "conduit", capable of routing the output to a recipient, but incapable of evaluating the output
* The skill of producing work and judging that work are deliberately distinct
  * Accomplishing work was used to teach judgement
  * Now, producing work belongs to machines
  * Judging work still belongs to humans, though the author contends that fewer people are bothering to acquire/utilize this skill
* The human in the agentic loop is the only part of the loop with skin in the game
* The pipeline of future experts is thinning from both ends
* A form of AI slop is time wasted on using AI on tasks that did not need it, on artifacts that no one will read, and on processes that only exist because a tool made it cheap to construct
* Discipline is now using LLMs where one can verify precisely what it produces
* Don't ask a model for confirmation - models will agree with everyone/everything
* The competitive advantage of a firm whose work can be trusted has not disappeared, it has appreciated

## [HackerNews Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48038001)
* The "productivity-gain bottleneck" is people who still care enough to review manually
* There will now be "experts" who have become so reliant/accustomed to AI that it dilutes their previously sharp judgement and taste
  * For example, needlessly verbose text written by someone who never had a habit of verbosity
  * Or a "trivial" problem that could be solved with a bash one-liner but now is a CLI with a storage backend, documentation, unit tests
  * Work is immune to "rational" criticism as it checks all the boxes: documentation, test coverage, etc
* Simulcra are copies that depict things that either had no original, or that no longer have an original
* Agentic code is probably releasing to production around now (5/8/2026)
* Commenter suspects that the most productive software teams will leverage LLMs to build quality software to 
  * Intelligent autocomplete
  * Brainstorming (LLMs can take a nebulous concept and expand on it in novel ways that can spark creativity)
  * Troubleshooting (LLMs can be useful when a developer is stuck and doesn't have a teammate to reach for)
  * Code review (akin to smarter linter)
  * POCs
  * Teams that go "all in" on agentic coding are going to inadvertently sabotage their product in the long run
* If people aren't aligned with the organization, then *bad* things happen when political people gain access to LLMs
  * These people can fake things for a very extended period of time, then find the most optimal way to cover up their failings before the consequences surface, at which point, they've already moved up the ladder and the consequences don't matter to them anymore
  * Commenter thinks that startups with star teams are going to absolutely dominate for a few years until near-full AI automation starts making big firms win again by virtue of throwing many tokens at the problem
* Because the cost of producing a document is now so low, low effort noise can masquerade as high effort signal
  * Now, can't just trust that because something looks high effort it actually is - direct relationships and knowledge about the contributor matter more
