# [Perfection is not over-engineering](https://var0.xyz/posts/perfection-is-not-over-engineering.html)
* Author contends that "over-engineering" is solving the wrong problem, not solving the right problem too well
* Answers to "why are things built the way they are?" are telling when understanding if/why certain systems were over-engineered

## [HackerNews Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48979120)
* [Commenter](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48989706) argues that in 99% of cases the architecture doesn't really matter because the product isn't ever going to need whatever fancy system exists because people overestimate scale
* Instead of "let's not make perfect the enemy of good", the idea is more like "don't let perfection be the enemy of completion"
* [Commenter](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48983879) argues that "perfect is the enemy of good" is used to justify shipping bad software much more often than it is used to prevent over-engineering something that is already in a good state
* [Author](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48979651) adds more context around the "why are things built this way?" question-as-a-heuristic (specifically, for a system with many microservices)
  * Answers the author received when asking this question were problems that the system did not have, like needing high availability, or state a problem that the system experienced but was solvable in a monolith
