# [Growing One's Consulting Business](https://training.kalzumeus.com/newsletters/archive/consulting_1)
* Don’t think of consulting as time-for-money but rather business-result-for-money
* Don’t think of yourself as a commodity provider for a well-understood service that is available in quantity and differentiated purely based on price

## Charging Weekly: It Makes Everything Automatically Better
* Weekly billing means never wasting time itemizing minute by minute invoices
* Weekly billing means there are uninterrupted consulting availbility chunks
  * Prospectiving, contract negotiations, etc. can happen between blocks rather than during the workday
* Weekly billing make it easier to align units of work to quantifiable business goals
* Author writes a proposal for an engagement by
  * Writing a list of tasks to execute
  * Estimating how many of those tasks will fit into 1, 2, 3 weeks
  * Author compromises on scope and not budget
* Quoting hourly rates encourages clients to see you as expensive and take a whack at the hourly rate (just to see if they can negotiate it down)
* Weekly rate discussions usually go like “You don’t have $X in the budget for Y weeks. Ok. What is your budget? $X - $A? Ok, what should be cut?”
  * Still preserves the ability to charge the full rate for the engagement and future engagements

## Getting Clients: The Importance Of Social Proof
* Always ask to follow a successful consulting engagement with a case study
  * Mutual win: client gets more exposure and consultant gets social proof, helping land the next client
* Write a testimonial after the engagement for them and ask if they want to make any changes to it
  * More persuasive testimonials demonstrate business value, and making the business more money than it costs
* Author jokes that each new case study adds a zero to the weekly rate
  * Directionally accurate as author’s first rate was $4k/week and at time of article it was several multiples of that
  * “XYZ client very similar to you paied my prevailing rate and had ABC fabulous result. If I could do that for you, what would it be worth?”
  * “My pipeline is sufficiently stuffed such that losing engagements over price shock is not a problem”
  * “Even at X% more than what I charge, I’m still confident that customers will have a successful outcome working with me”

## Scaling A Consulting Business
* Delivering for clients will lead to having too much work to handle
* Can either raise rates and/or turn away work in excess of your capacity to deliver or raise capacity, typically by hiring people
* Many folks working hourly succumb to the temptation of billing “just another hour” and end up with unideal work/life balance
* Having full-time employees can lead to “terrifying” cash flow swings where payroll checks need to clear on X date regardless of whether the customer has paid (or not)
* Contract employees out at a rate substantially higher than what you pay them, and then use the difference to build a cash-flow cushion
  * For example, have a $30k per employee cushion before hiring the next team member

## Hybridizing Consultancies With Product Businesses
* Use consulting revenues to underwrite product development
* Principals consult at generous rates, take modest salaries, and invest the difference in hiring employees at market rates
  * These employees build a product that the company then sells
  * Unlike bootstrapping a product from $0, everyone gets a paycheck every two weeks
  * Easier to close $20k in consulting contracts vs. $20k in angel investment
  * If product takes off, principals can stop consulting
* Think of repeated engagements and try to build a solution where there is near-zero marginal time investment
