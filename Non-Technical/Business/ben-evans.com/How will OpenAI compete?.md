# [How will OpenAI compete?](https://www.ben-evans.com/benedictevans/2026/2/19/how-will-openai-compete-nkg2x)
* Half-dozen organizations that are shipping competitive frontier models with relatively equivalent capabilities
  * There is no known mechanic for one company to gain an insurmountable lead overs in the field
  * Currently, no equivalent of network effects where market share was self-reinforcing such that no amount of money and effort was enough for someone to break in and catch up
* OpenAI has a clear lead in user base with 8-900 million users
  * These are weekly active users
  * Only 5% of ChatGPT users are paying
  * 80% of users sent less than 1,000 messages in 2025
* Most people aren't benefiting from memory
  * Memory is a product stickiness feature and not a network effect
* If people are only using this a couple of times a week, it hasn't been life-changing
  * OpenAI calls this a "capability gap", which the author contends is a way of describing lack of clear product-market fit
* OpenAI's ad project is partly about covering the cost of serving the 90% of users that don't pay, while also capturing an early lead with advertisers and learning how to deliver an advertising product within)
  * It's also about making it possible to give those non-paying users the most powerful (i.e. expensive) models in the hopes that these models will deepen their engagement
* Early leads in adoption for undifferentiated products tend to not be durable
  * This explains the rapid market share gains for Gemini and Meta AI
  * Has led people to compare ChatGPT to Netscape, where Microsoft used distribution to crowbar its way into a market where products were hard to tell apart
* Sam Altman presented a diagram where OpenAI was the platform powering third-party applications, with multiple layers in this platform - from ChatGPT to the underlying models to the underlying chips, data centers, and energy
  * This vertically-integrated approach generates a network effect where everybody is using your cloud, chips, models, etc

* Author is skeptical if OpenAI can achieve this network and flywheel effect
* Big four cloud companies spent $400 billion on infrastructure in 2025 and announced plans to spend at least $650 billion on infrastructure in 2026
* Rock's Law: the cost of a state-of-the-art semiconductor fab doubles every four years
* Author draws parallels between semiconductor manufacturing and generative AI where unit costs will fall, but fixed costs rise to a point where only a handful of companies are able to sustain the investment needed to build competitive models
* Recurring technological fallacy that one can abstract many different complex products into a simple standard interface - the "widget" fallacy
  * There's tension between providing a standardized abstraction layer and then a product owner's desire to control the experience with the customer
* OpenAI does not seem to have "power" - the ability to make people do something that they don't want to do
  * Author contends that OpenAI does not have the ability to get consumers, developers, and enterprises to use its systems more than anybody else (like a Microsoft, Apple, or Facebook)
